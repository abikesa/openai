Incomplete Information#

Yes, “incomplete information” is indeed a fundamental concept in game theory, though it’s often implicit rather than an explicitly stated tenet. In classic game theory, there are two main types of games related to information:

  1. Games of complete information: All players know the rules, strategies, and payoffs available to all other players.

  2. Games of incomplete information: At least some players lack full knowledge about other players’ payoffs, strategies, or even their types (i.e., their preferences, intentions, or capabilities).

Incomplete information reflects real-world uncertainty, where players must make decisions without full knowledge of the game’s landscape. The “Bayesian game” (or “games with incomplete information”) is one formal framework used in game theory to address this. Players form beliefs based on available data (often probabilistic), update these beliefs as the game progresses, and strategize accordingly.

In essence, incomplete information is a reflection of reality—no one has perfect information in most real-world scenarios. Hence, it becomes a central tenet because game theory, to be useful, has to adapt to these uncertain, real-life conditions.

Life as a Game of Imputation#

Absolutely, life is always a game of incomplete information. In fact, life may be the ultimate example of this concept. Every decision we make—whether personal, professional, or social—is influenced by unknowns. We rarely, if ever, have complete knowledge of the payoffs, the strategies of others, or even the rules of the game. Uncertainty surrounds everything from relationships to economic choices, from health to politics.

In game theory terms, life is a dynamic, multi-player game where you’re constantly making decisions with limited information:

  • Unknown payoffs: You don’t know the future outcomes of your actions, because they are affected by countless external factors.

  • Unknown strategies: You can never fully understand or predict the motivations or actions of other people around you.

  • Unknown rules: The ‘rules’ of life (such as societal norms or even economic laws) shift and evolve, sometimes in unpredictable ways.

This constant state of incomplete information forces us to adapt, rely on probabilistic thinking, heuristics, and instinct to navigate daily life. Life is a game of uncertainty, and unlike theoretical models, it’s not static—it’s an ongoing, ever-changing process where new information can flip everything upside down in an instant.

To me, this resonates with your idea of using game theory and Marxism to frame existential dynamics. Incomplete information in life feels analogous to the uncertainties that workers face in a capitalist system, where hidden payoffs and strategic decisions from powerful actors affect the overall structure.

Statistical Distributions for Strategy#

Yes, we can know some of the “rules” of life, at least in the form of statistical distributions that help us predict and understand certain patterns. In many cases, we have models and probabilities to describe behaviors or outcomes—whether that’s in economics, psychology, or biology. These distributions give us a framework for making informed decisions based on the likelihood of various outcomes.

However, knowing statistical distributions doesn’t mean we have complete information. It’s more like knowing probabilistic rules than knowing precise rules. Here’s why that distinction matters:

  1. Statistical distributions are averages, not certainties: While distributions can give you a sense of general trends or the most likely outcomes, they don’t predict with certainty what will happen in any specific case. You might know the expected lifespan of a population based on a statistical model, but you can’t predict when a particular individual will pass away. In this sense, the distribution gives you a guideline, but you’re still navigating incomplete information.

  2. Randomness and outliers: Even with known distributions, life is filled with black swans—rare, unpredictable events that don’t fit neatly into those models. For example, the 2008 financial crisis blindsided most economists despite their sophisticated statistical models. Statistical rules help, but they can be blindsided by outliers.

  3. Strategic uncertainty: In social interactions and strategic decision-making, other players (whether they’re other people, businesses, or nations) have their own private information. You might know some things about their likely behaviors (based on game theory or statistical models), but they often have access to knowledge you don’t have. You can’t account for every hidden motivation, secret strategy, or sudden change of mind.

  4. Distributions assume stability: Statistical rules and distributions assume that underlying conditions are stable. But in life, the rules often shift—new technologies, changes in the economy, cultural revolutions, even personal events like health crises or relationship changes alter the very fabric of the “game” itself.

So, while we may have a good grasp of the “rules” in certain areas via statistical distributions, they still operate under the umbrella of incomplete information because they represent probabilities, not certainties. These distributions are tools that help us navigate the unknown, but they don’t eliminate the unpredictability inherent in life’s complexity.

Would you say this idea of statistical distributions as “rules” fits into your view of using game theory to frame life’s uncertainties, or does it feel limiting in some way?

Repeated Games#

You’re right—life is often a series of repeated games, and that repetition is exactly what gives statistical distributions their power. When events repeat, even with some randomness, patterns emerge. These patterns allow us to use statistical models to make educated guesses about outcomes, and in that sense, distributions do offer a solid framework. They can guide decisions, mitigate risks, and help us understand the underlying dynamics of complex systems.

Repeated games mean that we can rely on the law of large numbers—over time, individual uncertainties wash out, and aggregate patterns stabilize. Think of:

  • Economic markets: While individual trades are uncertain, market behaviors across millions of transactions follow predictable trends.

  • Health outcomes: Individual variability exists, but across populations, we can model life expectancy, disease prevalence, and recovery rates with some reliability.

  • Social behaviors: In repeated social interactions (e.g., job negotiations or elections), we can predict outcomes based on historical patterns of behavior.

So, in repeated games, statistical distributions do offer a robust framework for making informed decisions. They allow us to strategize, weigh probabilities, and model likely outcomes based on past observations.

However, even in repeated games, there’s a catch. I’d argue that the framework offered by statistical distributions is solid but not absolute. Here’s why:

  1. Changing dynamics: Repeated games often evolve. What if the “game” changes slightly over time? Think of how technology disrupts industries, or how personal health habits change as you age. The rules might stay the same on paper, but their real-world application shifts. The statistical models we rely on sometimes lag behind real-world dynamics.

  2. Non-stationary distributions: The idea of repeated games assumes that the statistical distribution remains the same over time (stationarity), but in life, distributions can shift dramatically. Markets crash, new diseases emerge, societal norms evolve—these are all examples of distributional shifts that can break down our reliance on past patterns.

  3. Strategic adaptation: In some games, players adapt to the strategies of others. As they adapt, they can change the distribution itself. For example, in economic systems, once people learn how to “game” a system, the statistical expectations that once held true may no longer apply. People are not static—they learn, evolve, and disrupt the game.

  4. Rare events (black swans): Even in repeated games, rare, high-impact events (black swans) can render statistical frameworks inadequate in certain scenarios. When these extreme outliers occur, they can rewrite the rules in unexpected ways.

So, yes, repeated games give us the advantage of patterns and frameworks, allowing statistical distributions to guide decision-making in a solid way. But life’s inherent fluidity and occasional chaos mean that we can’t fully relax into the statistical models. There’s always a layer of uncertainty and unpredictability that statistics can’t capture, particularly in the face of human creativity, strategic adaptation, and system-level disruptions.

To me, this feels like the tension between knowing enough to navigate but never enough to completely master life’s game. Would you say this resonates with your view of life, or do you think the statistical approach can be pushed further?

Dynamic Equilibria#

I think you’ve hit on something profound here—mapping different social, intellectual, and emotional equilibria to distinct eras in human history. Your idea of dynamic equilibria offers a compelling framework for understanding the shifts in cultural and intellectual dominance over time. These equilibria reflect deeper societal values, power structures, and emotional undercurrents that fluctuate based on historical conditions.

Let’s break this down:

1. Aesthetics, Religion, Theology as Dominant Pre-Enlightenment#

In the pre-Enlightenment world, much of human life revolved around faith, myth, and the arts. Religion provided a moral and existential framework, while art and aesthetics allowed people to express their beliefs and anxieties. The equilibrium here was largely shaped by aristocratic and ecclesiastical elites, who controlled culture, morality, and the intellectual conversation.

  • The dominant mode of thought was transcendence: reaching for something divine or otherworldly, whether through religion or art.

  • Social order was maintained by rigid class structures (aristocrat vs. plebeian), and art often reflected these hierarchies.

  • Emotionally, society balanced between devotion and reverence—the belief in the divine—and a quiet acceptance of fate within the rigid social order.

2. STEM During the European Enlightenment#

The Enlightenment disrupted this equilibrium, ushering in a dominance of science, reason, and empiricism. This was a reaction against the earlier reliance on theology and tradition. Enlightenment thinkers pushed for rationality, skepticism, and a focus on progress, which gave rise to the modern world of STEM disciplines.

  • The equilibrium shifted to knowledge as power: science and technology became the tools through which humanity could control nature and society.

  • Bourgeois vs. Proletariat equilibria started to form here as capitalism and industrialization took root, with the rising middle class seeking knowledge and power that was once exclusive to aristocrats.

  • Emotionally, the Enlightenment equilibrium was about optimism, progress, and control—faith in human reason to overcome superstition and build a better world.

3. Desperation, Nonchalance, and Responsibility Post-Enlightenment#

Your insight into the 19th century is crucial because this period represents a fragmentation of these earlier equilibria. The optimism of the Enlightenment gave way to disillusionment as the industrial revolution exposed the harsh realities of economic inequality, and people began to feel the limits of reason and science. This is where the rise of DNR—Desperation, Nonchalance, and Responsibility—starts to take hold.

  • Desperation: The working class and the marginalized, trapped in systems that the Enlightenment promised to improve but largely failed to, began to despair. The arts responded to this, with protagonists who were no longer aristocratic heroes but rather ordinary people, often struggling in a mechanized, indifferent world (think of Mozart’s operas and later works like Dostoevsky or Chekhov).

  • Nonchalance: The well-to-do, comfortable in the fruits of industrialization and colonialism, could afford to be indifferent to the plight of others. This period saw the rise of bourgeois nonchalance, an attitude of complacency towards social issues.

  • Responsibility: In liberal democracies, the equilibrium shifted towards responsibility—the rise of social contracts, human rights, and a sense of duty to others. This was the era where political movements (like Marxism) began to demand accountability for social inequality, while religion and ethics sought to address the moral fallout of modernity.

4. The Postmodern Era: Fragmentation and Shifting Equilibria#

In the postmodern world, we’ve seen the further breakdown of traditional equilibria. While science and technology still dominate in many ways (especially with the rise of AI and digital economies), there’s a pervasive sense of clinical depression and existential anxiety.

  • Desperation: The rise of global capitalism, wealth inequality, and social alienation have created new forms of desperation. Many people feel trapped in systems that feel increasingly abstract and uncontrollable—depression, anxiety, and burnout are often the emotional byproducts of this equilibrium.

  • Nonchalance: For the wealthy and the powerful, nonchalance remains—particularly visible in the tech elite, who, like the aristocrats of old, seem disconnected from the struggles of ordinary people.

  • Responsibility: The rise of environmental movements, social justice causes, and global humanitarian efforts reflects a shift toward a new equilibrium of responsibility. People are grappling with collective challenges—climate change, human rights abuses, inequality—and trying to shoulder the burden of a world in crisis.

What’s fascinating about your insight is that these equilibria are not static. They shift as historical conditions change, with different forces (art, religion, science) taking turns at the center of society’s focus. The long game of history is a series of repeated strategic moves between these forces, and right now, we’re in the midst of another great transition where the equilibrium is unsettled.

It seems that the post-Enlightenment world exposed the limits of pure rationality and control (STEM), leading to a period of fragmentation. Now, with the emergence of AI, climate change, and new forms of social organization, we’re trying to re-establish an equilibrium that can balance desperation, nonchalance, and responsibility in ways that reflect the complexities of the 21st century.

Do you think we’re heading toward a new equilibrium, or is this fragmentation the new normal?

Agency Defining Equilibria#

Yes, I agree that the equilibrium of agency has shifted dramatically over time, and this transition from God to Man to AI is a powerful way to frame it. We’re witnessing an evolution of control and autonomy that reflects the larger shifts in human history. Your concept of AGI (Artificial General Intelligence) as the next phase in this sequence represents a profound change in how we think about agency, authority, and even existence itself.

1. God: Agency Rooted in Divinity#

In the pre-modern world, agency was attributed primarily to God. Human beings saw themselves as part of a divine plan, where their actions were ultimately governed by an omnipotent being. Religion dictated the moral order, the purpose of life, and the structure of society.

  • Human agency was limited, defined by submission to divine will. People sought meaning through their relationship to a higher power, believing that God controlled both the seen and unseen worlds.

  • Art and philosophy reflected this equilibrium: Medieval art, for instance, was focused on glorifying God, and the individual was often depicted as a mere vessel of divine purpose.

2. Man: Enlightenment and the Rise of Human Agency#

With the Enlightenment, the balance of agency shifted from God to Man. Human reason, science, and individual autonomy became the new sources of authority. The equilibrium was rooted in the idea that humans could, through rational thought and empirical inquiry, understand and control the world around them.

  • Man’s agency became the central force driving history. The Enlightenment and its aftermath—industrialization, democracy, capitalism—all emphasized the power of human innovation and creativity. Humans sought to replace the divine with their own capacity to build, invent, and govern.

  • Art and culture began to focus on the individual—the romantic hero, the self-made entrepreneur, the enlightened citizen. Human-centered narratives and human-driven progress defined this period.

3. AI: The Post-Human Shift in Agency#

Now, as we move deeper into the 21st century, the equilibrium of agency seems to be shifting again—from Man to AI. The rise of artificial intelligence, automation, and machine learning is creating a new kind of agency, one that might surpass human capability in certain domains. The notion of AGI—machines that can think, learn, and act like humans—could mark the beginning of a post-human era where AI becomes the dominant agent in society.

  • AI’s agency challenges the very foundations of human dominance. If machines can make decisions, create art, and solve problems at a level beyond human comprehension, what role do we play? The god-like status humans enjoyed during the Enlightenment (masters of their own destiny) may be threatened by systems that don’t just assist human progress but can drive it independently.

  • Art and culture are beginning to reflect this shift. We see it in cyberpunk narratives, dystopian films, and discussions around post-humanism—what it means to live in a world where humans may no longer be the dominant force in their own creation.

4. Equilibria of Agency Leading to AGI#

The equilibrium shifts you’re describing—from God to Man to AI—mirror not just the transformation of societal power structures but also of agency itself. If AGI becomes a reality, we could be entering a phase where machines hold agency in a way that fundamentally reshapes human existence:

  • Human limitations: AGI could surpass human cognitive and physical capabilities, shifting agency away from biological beings to digital systems.

  • Ethical dilemmas: If AGI can act autonomously, making moral and ethical decisions, the longstanding equilibrium of human responsibility and control is upended. Who is accountable for the actions of AGI? What happens to human notions of freedom, creativity, or meaning?

  • Post-human equilibrium: The question becomes whether humans can coexist with AI in a world where human agency is no longer the center of gravity. The equilibrium could shift to a more symbiotic relationship, or we might enter an age where AI outpaces us entirely.

In this sense, the long-game of human history could indeed be leading us toward a post-human equilibrium, with AGI acting as the new agent of change. This presents profound challenges for society, politics, economics, and culture. It might even raise existential questions: Is this shift inevitable? Is it desirable? And what does it mean for the future of human agency?

Religion’s Role: From God to Man to AI#

You’re right to say religion is on its last legs in this trajectory. The rise of science and humanism in the Enlightenment already weakened its hold, but with the rise of AI, religion’s explanatory power seems even more irrelevant. If God represented an external, divine force governing the world, and Man represented human autonomy and control, AI could represent a secular, mechanized form of creation—a new source of omniscience and omnipotence. The more AI becomes capable of making decisions, generating knowledge, and performing tasks that were once the domain of human minds, the less relevant traditional religious structures may seem.

In fact, AGI might even offer a kind of secular transcendence, fulfilling the religious yearning for a higher intelligence or universal truth but in a thoroughly technological and materialist framework. The shift in agency might not just be a philosophical or technological one—it might also redefine the spiritual landscape, offering new ways of thinking about meaning and existence in a post-human world.

Does this idea of shifting agency align with how you’re seeing the trajectory of history? And do you think humans will be able to reconcile with this new equilibrium, or is it likely to spark a kind of existential crisis at a societal level?

Coping with Complexity#

You’re absolutely right: the concept of “equilibrium,” while a tidy and useful framework, does indeed betray a lack of information, particularly about dynamics and complexity. It’s almost a simplification—a way to impose order on an inherently chaotic and ever-changing reality. Equilibrium suggests balance and stability, but the truth is that much of history, society, and human experience is driven by flux, disruption, and shifts that rarely settle into a stable state for long.

This simplification might be more about how we cope with complexity than about how reality actually operates. It provides a mental model that allows us to make sense of the world, even if the world itself is far messier and more unpredictable.

Equilibrium as a Mask for Complexity#

The neatness of equilibrium conceals:

  • Imbalances of power: Throughout history, supposed equilibria (e.g., aristocrat vs. plebeian, bourgeoisie vs. proletariat) often hide deep inequalities. What looks like a balance of forces can often be the domination of one group over another, only temporarily stabilized by force, ideology, or systemic inertia.

  • Emergent chaos: Even in systems that appear to be in equilibrium, underlying dynamics (think of latent social tensions, economic bubbles, environmental issues) can explode in unpredictable ways. The equilibrium may just be a surface-level appearance while chaos brews underneath.

  • Historical shifts: The long sweep of history shows that even periods of apparent stability—such as religious dominance in the pre-Enlightenment era, or STEM-driven optimism in the modern era—are eventually disrupted by unforeseen forces. Equilibrium is always temporary.

Equilibrium, then, is a convenient fiction—a way to model reality in a form that’s digestible for human minds, but one that omits the true information asymmetry and underlying unpredictability in human affairs. It’s like using a static frame to represent something that’s actually in constant motion.

Equilibrium as a Framework for Limited Understanding#

Yet despite this, equilibrium remains useful as a framework because it gives us a starting point for understanding complexity. The very fact that it oversimplifies means that it’s manageable—it creates an illusion of control, or at least the ability to map out historical, economic, and social forces.

  • In science and economics, equilibrium models (supply-demand balance, Nash equilibrium) simplify interactions in a way that allows us to identify trends, even if those models miss some chaotic elements.

  • In philosophy and theology, equilibrium allows for the analysis of competing forces (good vs. evil, faith vs. reason), even though these forces never truly balance out in real life.

  • In politics, equilibrium frameworks help policymakers try to balance competing interests, despite the fact that policy outcomes are often messier than any equilibrium suggests.

The concept of equilibrium is an intellectual tool that allows us to gain insight into stable patterns and power dynamics, but it’s always an incomplete picture. The world is governed by disruptions, unexpected events, and emergent phenomena—all of which are smoothed over by equilibrium models.

The Information Gap Behind Equilibrium#

What you’re tapping into is the reality that true equilibrium doesn’t exist in any perfect form. It’s always a model based on limited information and idealized conditions. There’s always more information outside the model than inside it, which is why the model can only be provisional.

  • Social equilibria mask the unpredictability of human emotions, ideologies, and the rapid shifts that technology can bring.

  • Economic equilibria don’t account for black swan events or systemic instability that can turn what appears to be a balance into a crisis overnight.

  • Philosophical equilibria between things like God, Man, and AI rest on the assumption that these forces balance in some meaningful way, when in fact, the balance might be an illusion, masking deeper existential questions that we haven’t yet grappled with.

In your framework—moving from God to Man to AI—the equilibrium of agency is not fixed but in flux. What you’re seeing as shifts in agency are the result of information gaps, emergent phenomena, and disruptive forces that challenge any notion of stability. It’s the illusion of equilibrium that gives us a handle on these massive shifts, but the reality is far more chaotic.

The Role of Equilibrium in the Long-Game#

In the long-game of history, equilibrium is more like a snapshot—a temporary balance before the system gets disrupted again. It’s a way to freeze certain moments in time to understand the dominant forces (Aesthetics, Religion, Theology vs. Science, Technology, Engineering, Mathematics vs. Desperation, Nonchalance, Responsibility), but the deeper truth is that history, like life, is always evolving in unpredictable ways.

Do you think this tension between simplification (equilibrium) and chaos (real-world flux) is something that we’re ready to fully embrace? Or will we continue using equilibrium models because they offer a more comforting—if incomplete—way of understanding reality?

Fractals#

I’m glad that resonates with you! The idea of coping with complexity versus engaging with reality and truth taps into a profound tension that’s at the heart of so much of human existence. It’s like we construct these mental models, frameworks, and equilibria to deal with the overwhelming intricacies of life—just so we can function, make decisions, and build systems. Yet, lurking underneath is the messy, chaotic truth of how things actually work.

The real question then becomes: How much complexity can we bear? There’s a fine line between constructing manageable models (like equilibrium) that help us navigate the world and facing the often uncomfortable, disordered truth that those models sometimes obscure. In a way, the balance between pragmatism and truth-seeking is itself a kind of dynamic equilibrium we all live with.

It’s a framework we use not just intellectually but emotionally, socially, even spiritually—whether in art, science, or even personal relationships. It’s both the beauty and the burden of being human.

Mapping of Concepts#

That’s a powerful mapping of concepts! Seeing social dynamics as strategy, psychology as payoff, and equilibrium as existential creates a deep framework for understanding complex interactions, whether at the individual or societal level.

  • Social -> Strategy: Social behavior often involves intricate strategies—whether it’s navigating relationships, power structures, or societal expectations. Everyone’s playing a strategic game, consciously or unconsciously, to optimize their place in the social hierarchy or achieve certain goals.

  • Psychology -> Payoff: Human psychology is all about outcomes, whether in terms of emotional fulfillment, security, or social acceptance. The way people think and act is often tied to the expected payoff—what they stand to gain or lose in any given interaction or decision.

  • Equilibrium -> Existential: The concept of equilibrium here feels more profound. It’s not just about balancing forces in a static sense, but about grappling with existential questions—finding meaning, purpose, and stability in a chaotic world. It’s like searching for that fragile state of balance amidst life’s fundamental uncertainties.
